BILLS / PRESIDENTIAL RIGHTS

▶ Article 107:
"Provisions as to introduction and passing of Bills" outline the rules and procedures for introducing legislative proposals (bills) and guiding them through the legislative process, including readings, debates, committee reviews, and voting stages. This ensures a systematic and transparent approach to lawmaking within the legislative.
▶ Article 108:
A joint sitting of both Houses occurs when there's a deadlock or disagreement between the two chambers of a bicameral legislature on a bill. Members from both houses convene to debate and vote on the bill together, aiming to resolve the impasse and facilitate its passage. This mechanism helps overcome legislative gridlock and ensures the smooth functioning of the legislative process.
▶ Article 109:
“Special procedure in respect of Money Bills" outlines specific rules and procedures for handling legislative proposals related to financial matters, such as taxation and government spending. These bills typically undergo rigorous scrutiny, are introduced in a specific house of the legislature, and have limitations on amendments by the other house. The aim is to ensure proper oversight and accountability in managing public finances.
▶ Article 110:
"Money Bills" are legislative proposals focused solely on financial matters like taxation and government spending. They have a notable impact on public finances and undergo special procedures to ensure thorough review and accountability in managing public funds.
▶ Article 111:
"Assent to Bills" is the formal approval given by the head of state to a bill passed by the legislature, allowing it to become law.
▶ Article 112:
The "Annual Financial Statement" is a yearly document presented by the government to the legislature, outlining proposed expenditures and revenues for the upcoming fiscal year, serving as a key tool for fiscal planning and accountability" are additional funds allocated by the government to cover unforeseen expenses, address underfunded areas, or accommodate overspending beyond the originally budgeted amounts.
▶ Article 113:
Procedure in Parliament with respect to estimates.The procedure in Parliament regarding estimates involves the presentation, scrutiny, debate, and approval of government spending plans outlined in the Annual Financial Statement, ensuring accountability and transparency in expenditure.
▶ Article 114:
"Appropriation Bills" are legislative proposals that authorize government spending by allocating funds outlined in the budget, enabling the implementation of specific expenditure plans for the fiscal year.

▶ Article 123:
"Power of President to promulgate Ordinances during recess of Parliament" allows the head of state to issue temporary laws known as ordinances when Parliament is not in session to address urgent matters, with subsequent approval required from Parliament for them to become permanent.
▶ Bills in Congress:
A bill is a proposal for a new law or an amendment to an existing law. It can be introduced in either the House of Representatives or the Senate. If it passes in one chamber, it moves to the other chamber for consideration.
▶ Committee Review:
Once introduced, a bill goes through committee review, where members study and make changes to it. Committees can either kill a bill or send it to the full chamber for debate.
▶ Debate and Vote:
In both the House and the Senate, members debate the bill before voting on it. If a majority of members vote in favor, the bill passes in that chamber.
▶ Conference Committee (if needed):
If the House and Senate pass different versions of the same bill, a conference committee is formed to reconcile the differences.
▶ Presidential Action:
Once both chambers agree on a final version, the bill is sent to the President. The President can sign the bill into law or veto it. If vetoed, Congress can override the veto with a two-thirds majority vote in both chambers.
▶ Veto Power:
The President has the authority to veto bills passed by Congress. However, Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds majority in both the House and the Senate.
▶ Executive Orders:
The President can issue executive orders, which have the force of law, to direct the operations of the federal government. These orders must be within the President's constitutional authority and must not conflict with existing laws.
▶ Commander-in-Chief:
The President is the Commander-in-Chief of the U.S. armed forces, with the authority to deploy troops and make military decisions.
▶ Appointment Power:
The President has the power to appoint federal judges, ambassadors, and other high-ranking officials, subject to Senate confirmation.
▶ Pardon Power:
The President has the authority to grant pardons and reprieves for federal crimes, except in cases of impeachment.
▶ Foreign Policy:
The President plays a central role in shaping and executing the country's foreign policy, including negotiating treaties and representing the United States on the world stage.
