Parliament

▶ Article 79:
This article deals with the constitution of Parliament. It states that there shall be a Parliament for the Union, which shall consist of the President and two Houses, known as the Council of States (Rajya Sabha) and the House of the People (Lok Sabha).
▶ Article 80:
This article deals with the composition of the Council of States (Rajya Sabha). It specifies that the Rajya Sabha shall consist of representatives of the States and of the Union territories.
▶ Article 81:
This article deals with the composition of the House of the People (Lok Sabha). It specifies the allocation of seats in the House of the People, which are to be filled by representatives elected directly by the people.
▶ Article 82:
This article deals with the duration of the Houses of Parliament. It specifies that the House of the People (Lok Sabha) shall continue for five years from the date of its first meeting, unless dissolved earlier.
▶ Article 83:
This article deals with the duration of the Houses of Parliament. It specifies that the Council of States (Rajya Sabha) shall not be subject to dissolution, but one-third of its members shall retire every two years.
▶ Article 84:
This article deals with qualifications for membership of Parliament. It specifies the qualifications required for a person to be chosen as a member of either House of Parliament.
▶ Article 85:
This article deals with the sessions of Parliament, prorogation and dissolution. It empowers the President to summon each House of Parliament to meet at such time and place as he thinks fit.
▶ Article 86:
This article deals with the right of the President to address and send messages to Houses of Parliament.
▶ Article 87:
This article deals with special address by the President. It states that the President may address either House of Parliament or both Houses assembled together.
▶ Article 88:
This article deals with the rights of Ministers and Attorney-General as to speak in and otherwise take part in the proceedings of Parliament.
▶ Article 89:
This article deals with the money bills. It specifies that a money bill can only be introduced in the House of the People (Lok Sabha).
▶ Article 90:
This article deals with the provisions as to introduction and passing of bills. It describes the process through which bills are passed by the Houses of Parliament.
▶ Article 91:
This article deals with the powers of the Parliament in respect of the subjects enumerated in the Union List.
▶ Article 92:
This article deals with the constitution of the House of Parliament and the powers of the Houses.
▶ Article 93:
This article deals with the vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the offices of Speaker and Deputy Speaker, and the Speaker's certificate of money bills.
▶ Article 94:
This article deals with the Deputy Speaker and other presiding officers of the House of the People.
▶ Article 95:
This article deals with the Speaker's powers to adjourn the House or to suspend the meeting thereof.
▶ Article 96:
This article deals with the provision for the Deputy Chairman of the Council of States.
▶ Article 97:
This article deals with the provision for the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Council of States.
▶ Article 98:
This article deals with the provision for the Rules of procedure.
▶ The Representation of the People Act, 1951:
This act provides for the conduct of elections to the Houses of Parliament and to the Legislative Assemblies of States.
▶ The Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1959:
This act specifies certain offices or positions that do not disqualify a person from being chosen as, or being, a member of either House of Parliament.
▶ The Salaries and Allowances of Ministers Act, 1952:
This act provides for the payment of salaries and allowances to Ministers and Deputy Ministers in Parliament and State Legislatures
▶ The Salary, Allowances and Pension of Members of Parliament Act, 1954:
This act provides for the payment of salaries and allowances to Members of Parliament (MPs).
▶ The Parliament (Facilities) Act, 1998:
This act provides for facilities to be accorded to the Leaders of the Opposition in Parliament.
▶ The Parliament (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1971:
This act provides for the prevention of atrocities and the protection of Parliament's dignity.
▶ The Parliament (Prevention of Publication of Unauthorized Papers) Act, 1977:
This act prohibits the publication of unauthorized reports of parliamentary proceedings.
▶ The Parliament (Declaration of Qualifications) Act, 1959:
This act declares certain qualifications for membership of Parliament.
▶ The Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2003:
This amendment act brought significant changes to the electoral laws, including provisions related to electronic voting machines, disqualification of MPs and MLAs, and voter identification.
▶ The Anti-Defection Law (The Tenth Schedule of the Constitution):
This schedule, inserted by the Fifty-Second Amendment Act, 1985, deals with the disqualification of Members on the ground of defection.
▶ The Parliamentary Standing Committee's Rules, 1964:
These rules lay down the procedures and functioning of the various parliamentary standing committees.
▶ The Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business in Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha:
These rules govern the conduct of business in the two Houses of Parliament, covering various aspects such as debates, questions, motions, voting, and legislative procedures.
